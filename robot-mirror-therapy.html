Robot Mirror Therapy

Developed a lightweight exoskeleton for children with hemiplegic cerebral palsy, integrating mirror therapy techniques to improve motor function and rehabilitation outcomes.

Role

Developer & Researcher

Date

June - Aug 2022

Tools

Arduino, MATLAB, Python, Motor Control Systems, 3D Printing, SolidWorks

Skills

Biomechanics, Control Systems, Rehabilitation Engineering, Exoskeleton Design, Product Development, Team Collaboration

Overview

Hemiplegic cerebral palsy (CP) is a condition that affects one side of a child's body, limiting mobility and muscle function. This project sought to address these challenges through an innovative combination of mirror therapy and robotic assistive technology, creating a functional exoskeleton to aid rehabilitation.

Problem Statement


Children with hemiplegic CP often struggle with muscle stiffness, poor control, and limited mobility, which can hinder everyday activities. Traditional mirror therapy helps to train neural pathways but lacks sufficient physical engagement. This project aimed to enhance the therapyâ€™s effectiveness by integrating robotic assistance to provide active, repetitive rehabilitation exercises.

Solutions


  • Combine Mirror Therapy: Provide visual feedback to enhance neural pathway training.
  • Assistive Technology: Utilize motorized assistance for the impaired arm and sensorized tracking for the unimpaired arm.
  • Adjustable Design: Include modular components for scalability and ease of use for children.

Impact


  • Enhanced Rehabilitation: Improved muscle engagement and motor function through active and passive exercises.
  • Child-Centric Design: Aimed to make therapy engaging and effective for young users.

  1. Research & Planning: Studied hemiplegic CP and existing therapeutic methods, focusing on the limitations of traditional mirror therapy and the potential benefits of robotics in rehabilitation.
  2. Design & Prototyping:
    • Initial Prototypes: Developed a dual-arm exoskeleton with motorized assistance for the impaired arm and sensors for the unimpaired arm.
    • Iteration: Refined the design based on testing, ensuring safety, usability, and adaptability for children.
  3. Implementation & Testing:
    • Integrated Sensors and Motors: Combined Arduino-based motor control with feedback systems for real-time adjustments.
    • Validation: Tested the exoskeleton on simulated models to ensure reliability and effectiveness.

Process & Methodology

Final Product

This is the final build of our exoskeleton arm. The impaired arm (right side) is motorized and the unimpaired arm (left side) is just sensorized. There are divots in the bar so that forearm rests, handles, etc. can be placed easily, without the need to redesign the exoskeleton. The person will put their arms in the respective locations and will be strapped in. Once the arm is secure the person can then move their unimpaired arm. When the unimpaired arm moves, the sensors will trigger the motor to move, causing the impaired arm to mirror the movements of the unimpaired arm. The movement that this build can do is only elbow flexion and extension. In future iterations, wrist pronation and supination can be added.
